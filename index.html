About Me

I am a Postdoctoral Researcher in organizational & strategy theory at Aarhus University's Department of Business Development and Technology. Drawing on organizational search theory, cognitive science, and complexity economics, I currently study three intertwined ideas:

  1. Cognitive diversity as strategic asset — why heterogeneity in mental models and heuristics helps groups avoid early convergence and climb higher peaks.
  2. Mental-model formation and generalisation — how people infer the shape of an unknown landscape and how the breadth of those inferences governs the balance between distant exploration and local refinement.
  3. Emergent meta-heuristics — why many successful groups spontaneously adopt a simulated-annealing pattern (early wide search, later focused search) and how this pattern can be engineered or mimicked.

To investigate these mechanisms I combine behavioural experiments and computational models. In test theory I use the gamified platform Crystal Crop Fever to capture fine-grained trajectories of human search, and to develop new theory, I build agent-based models. This dual approach allows me to move iteratively between data and theory: experiments reveal unexpected regularities; models explain why they appear and when they should break down.

My route to organizational & strategy theory began when I studied physics at Master's level, where I learned that tiny design choices can reshape complex systems — a lesson that now informs my work on organizations. Industry experience as a Data Scientist at the Center for Hybrid Intelligence taught me to translate abstract ideas into tools people actually use, such as the citizen-science games Quantum Moves and Skill Lab.
